Decision Guide

The 5,000 Rule — A Simple Starting Point

Before anything else, apply this straightforward calculation used by HVAC professionals: multiply your system's age in years by the cost of the repair being quoted. If the result exceeds $5,000, replacement deserves serious consideration. If it's well under $5,000 and the system is otherwise healthy, repair almost always makes sense.

Example: A 10-year-old furnace needs a $300 igniter replacement. 10 × $300 = $3,000. Repair is the clear choice. A 17-year-old furnace needs a $600 heat exchanger repair. 17 × $600 = $10,200. Replacement is likely the right decision — the system is past its expected lifespan and a heat exchanger failure often signals the beginning of the end.

When to Repair Your Furnace

System Is Under 10 Years Old

Furnaces under 10 years old have most of their serviceable life ahead of them. Even moderately expensive repairs are usually justified. The system is likely running at or near its rated efficiency, and replacement would mean absorbing the full cost of a new unit unnecessarily.

Repair Cost Is Under $500

Minor repairs — flame sensor cleaning, capacitor, igniter, pressure switch, thermocouple — are almost always worth making regardless of age. These are consumable components that wear out normally and don't indicate systemic failure.

System Has Been Well-Maintained

A furnace that has received annual tune-ups will outlast one that hasn't, and will give more warning when it's in decline. If your system has a clean maintenance history and the repair is isolated, repair is the right call.

When to Replace Your Furnace

System Is 15+ Years Old

Most gas furnaces have a lifespan of 15 to 20 years. Past 15 years, you're in the zone where any major repair could be followed closely by another — and another. Ohio winters put real load on heating systems. At this age, weigh repair cost against the fact that replacement may be inevitable within a few years anyway.

Frequent Breakdowns

One breakdown is a repair. Two or three in a single season is a pattern. When a system starts failing regularly, it's telling you something. The cumulative cost of repeated repairs often exceeds what a replacement would have cost, without the reliability a new system provides.

Rising Energy Bills

An aging furnace loses efficiency — especially if it hasn't been maintained. An older 80% AFUE furnace uses 20 cents of every dollar of gas to heat your home inefficiently. Modern high-efficiency furnaces operate at 95 to 98% AFUE. In an Ohio winter, that difference shows up on your gas bill every month.

Cracked Heat Exchanger

A cracked heat exchanger is effectively a furnace death sentence. It's a safety hazard — combustion gases can enter your home's air supply — and repairing it costs nearly as much as a new unit. In most cases, replacement is the only sensible path.

Repair vs. Replace: Air Conditioners

Central AC units typically last 12 to 17 years. Apply the same 5,000 rule. One additional factor specific to AC: if your system uses R-22 refrigerant (phased out as of 2020), refrigerant is now extremely expensive and difficult to source. A system that needs R-22 added is one where replacement becomes financially compelling regardless of age.

Repair vs. Replace: Heat Pumps

Heat pumps run year-round — both heating and cooling — which shortens their expected lifespan to 12 to 15 years compared to a dedicated furnace or AC. They're also more complex, which makes major component failures (compressor, reversing valve) more expensive to repair. A heat pump over 12 years old needing a compressor replacement is almost always a candidate for full replacement.

Clear Signs It's Time to Replace

  • System is 15+ years old (furnace) or 12+ years old (AC/heat pump)
  • You've had two or more breakdowns in one season
  • Repair cost × system age exceeds $5,000
  • Cracked heat exchanger diagnosed
  • System uses R-22 refrigerant and needs recharging
  • Energy bills have increased significantly without explanation
  • System can no longer maintain comfortable temperatures

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does a furnace last in Ohio?

A gas furnace in Ohio typically lasts 15 to 20 years with regular annual maintenance. Ohio's cold winters mean heating systems work hard from November through March, which accelerates wear. Systems that haven't been maintained regularly tend to fall on the shorter end of that range.

How long does a central AC unit last?

Central air conditioners typically last 12 to 17 years. In Ohio's climate, where AC runs hard through July and August, systems on the lower end of that range are common when maintenance has been inconsistent. Heat pumps that run year-round for both heating and cooling tend to have shorter lifespans — around 12 to 15 years.

Is it worth repairing a 20-year-old furnace?

Generally no, unless the repair is minor and inexpensive. A 20-year-old furnace is well past its expected lifespan, likely operating at 60 to 70 percent efficiency compared to 95 to 98 percent for modern high-efficiency units. The energy savings from replacement often offset the cost within 5 to 7 years, and you eliminate the risk of a major failure during a cold Ohio winter.

What is the 5,000 rule for HVAC?

Multiply your system's age in years by the estimated repair cost. If the result exceeds $5,000, replacement is typically the better financial decision. For example: a 12-year-old furnace needing a $450 repair gives you 5,400 — borderline, but the repair likely makes sense if the system is otherwise in good condition. A 16-year-old unit needing a $400 repair gives 6,400 — replacement is worth considering.

Does a new HVAC system add value to a home in Ohio?

Yes, though the ROI varies. A new HVAC system is a strong selling point in Ohio's climate — buyers know a functioning, efficient system saves them an immediate large expense. Appraisers typically assign value to newer systems. More practically, a failing or outdated system can give buyers negotiating leverage or cause deals to fall through during inspection.
